New forest fire detector that recharges the battery with the movement of branches

surely on more than one occasion you have touched a person's hair, or an article of clothing, and you have suffered a small discharge. This is what is known as the triboelectric effect. This effect produces a small discharge when rubbing two materials with different charges and polarities. Based on this, researchers at Michigan State University have developed a fire detector called MC-TENG, made up of multiple cylinders of materials that, when rubbed together, generate electricity. Some of these cylinders are attached to the tree, and the rest are attached to the branches. When the wind blows the branches move, causing the cylinders to face each other to produce an electric shock. In the first tests they have achieved that, with only three minutes of wind not too strong, the detector gets enough energy to carry out a fire check and send a warning if it detects fire.
